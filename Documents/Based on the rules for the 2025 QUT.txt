Based on the rules for the 2025 QUT Droid Racing Challenge, your robot will need a software architecture heavily focused on computer vision and autonomous decision-making.

Here is a detailed, section-by-section guide outlining the core coding components your team will need to develop:

1. Wireless Communication & State Management
This component acts as the master switch for the robot's logic, handling the transition between idle, racing, and finished states.

The robot must be able to be started, stopped, and finished wirelessly via a handheld remote.  

The robot must operate entirely autonomously during the race, as any manual remote control of the driving will result in disqualification.  

You should include logic to stop the droid autonomously once its nose reaches the start/finish line after a lap, as this grants a 5-second time bonus.  

2. Computer Vision (CV) Engine
Because main guidance must be strictly computer vision-based, this will be your most intensive processing module.

Primary guidance must rely on computer vision using on-board sensors, as GPS, RC, and LiDAR are strictly prohibited.  

The CV system must identify yellow tape to determine the track's left boundary and blue tape to determine the right boundary.  

The system needs to detect green tape, which indicates the start/finish line, direction arrows, and track completion markers.  

The vision pipeline must locate and interpret a black arrow sign measuring approximately 28x20cm that dictates upcoming left or right turns.  

3. Obstacle & Entity Recognition
This section handles the identification of dynamic and static hazards on the track so the navigation system can plot a safe course.

For teams in the Opens category, the vision system must detect purple obstacles that can be up to 400x400x500mm in size.  

The robot must use its vision to sense and avoid other droids on the track.  

The detection models need to be robust enough to ignore background visual noise, such as spectators or judges wearing clothing that matches the colors of the track lines or obstacles.  

4. Autonomous Navigation & Path Planning
This is the "brain" of the robot. It takes the parsed data from the CV engine and computes the optimal path forward.

Your pathfinding algorithm must calculate a trajectory that keeps the robot strictly within the track boundaries to avoid crossing the tape and incurring 2, 5, or 10-second time penalties.  

Upon detecting the turning challenge arrow, the software must route the robot to the indicated left or right fork.  

Successfully executing the correct turn yields a 5-second bonus.  

In the Opens category, taking the wrong turn results in a 5-second penalty, and failing to choose a direction altogether causes a 10-second penalty.  

You are permitted to use any software, and processing can be handled either on-board or off-board.  

5. Motor Control Interface (Actuation)
This final piece of the software stack translates the digital path planning into physical hardware movements.

This section translates the calculated paths into propulsion and steering signals for the wheels, ensuring the robot remains on the ground at all times.  

The control loop should actively account for traction and surface changes, particularly at the turning challenge where the surface grip may increase or decrease over a slightly raised area.
